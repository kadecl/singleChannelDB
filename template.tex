In room acoustic environments, the signal recorded by a microphone can be modeled as the linear convolution of the room impulse response (RIR) $\bm{h} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ with a dry signal that does not include reverberation:
\begin{equation}
  \bm{x} = \bm{s} \ast \bm{h}
\end{equation}

By introducing the convolution matrix $\mathbf{M}_l(\bm{a})$, the above equation can be rewritten as:
\[
\bm{s} \ast \bm{h} = \mathbf{M}_d(\bm{s}) \bm{h}
\]

Now, suppose we have two different signals $\bm{s}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{l_1}$ 
and $\bm{s}_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{l_2}$, both convolved with the same RIR, resulting in:
\begin{align}
  \bm{x}_1 = \bm{s}_1 \ast \bm{h} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_1} \\
  \bm{x}_2 = \bm{s}_2 \ast \bm{h} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_2}
\end{align}

Then, the following relationship holds:
\begin{multline}
  \bm{x}_1 \ast \bm{s}^2 = \bm{s}^1 \ast \bm{h} \ast \bm{s}^2 = \bm{x}^2 \ast \bm{s}^1 \\
  \iff \bm{x}^1 \ast \bm{s}^2 - \bm{x}^2 \ast \bm{s}^1 = \bm{0} \\
  \iff [\mathbf{M}_{l_2}(\bm{x}^2)\, \mathbf{M}_{l_2}(\bm{x}^1)]
  \begin{bmatrix}\bm{s}^1\\ -\bm{s}^2\end{bmatrix} = \bm{0}
\end{multline}

The matrix appearing in the last row of the above equation is known as 
the \textit{d-th subresultant Sylvester matrix} $\Syl_d(\bm{x}^1, \bm{x}^2)$.
If $\bm{s}^1$ and $\bm{s}^2$ are coprime?-meaning they are not convolutions of the same vector?the vector that annihilates $\Syl_d$ is uniquely determined up to scalar  multiplication, as shown in the equation above.
Therefore, the dry signals $\bm{s}^1$ and $\bm{s}^2$ can be recovered as the right  singular vector corresponding to the unique zero singular value.

However, the Sylvester matrix often becmoes huge size in practical situation.
For example, if the length of the dry signals and RIR are 1 sec 20kHz of sampling frequency,　the size of Sylvester matrix is X by Y, occupying more than 16GB in double floating point accuracy.
This quantity is too large for personal computer and users cannot run the program.

In recent years, methods that model reverberation as a linear convolution in the short-time Fourier transform (STFT) domain have shown promising results \cite{wpe, yohana2025}.
Specifically, letting f denote the frequency bin index, it is assumed that the STFT of the reverberant (wet) signal can be expressed as
\begin{equation}
  X_f \approx S_f \ast H_f,
\end{equation}
where X_f, S_f, and H_f represent the STFTs of the observed signal, the dry signal, and the room impulse response, respectively.
